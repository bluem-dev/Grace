# Grace Design Principles

Version: v1.0
Classification: Normative
Role: Operational Principles Layer

---

# 1. Introduction

Grace implementations may differ.

Technologies may evolve.

Execution environments may change.

The following principles are invariant.

Any system that violates these principles may use the name Grace, but it is no longer operating as Grace.

---

# Principle 01 — Canonical Reality

At any given moment, only one authoritative state may exist for a given artifact.

Multiple drafts may coexist.

Multiple canonical states may not.

Canonical reality is mandatory for continuity.

---

# Principle 02 — Explicit Closure

Work must possess a recognizable completion state.

Artifacts may evolve after closure.

Artifacts may not remain permanently undefined.

Open work accumulates uncertainty.

Closed work creates stability.

---

# Principle 03 — Artifact Identity Preservation

Artifacts must preserve identity throughout their lifecycle.

Modification does not create replacement.

Evolution does not destroy lineage.

Every artifact must remain traceable to its origin.

---

# Principle 04 — Traceable Decisions

Meaningful decisions must be explainable.

Artifacts must preserve:

* rationale,
* assumptions,
* constraints,
* consequences.

Decisions without traceability cannot be governed.

---

# Principle 05 — Governed Mutation

Changes may occur only through explicit mutation processes.

Uncontrolled modification creates fragmentation.

Every mutation must preserve:

* consistency,
* lineage,
* ownership,
* recoverability.

---

# Principle 06 — Reconciliation Before Replacement

Conflicting states must first attempt reconciliation.

Replacement becomes acceptable only when reconciliation fails.

Destruction is cheaper than reconciliation.

Grace intentionally prefers the more expensive path.

---

# Principle 07 — Deterministic Realization

Equivalent context and equivalent intent should produce equivalent outcomes.

Perfect determinism may be impossible.

Behavioral consistency is mandatory.

Trust emerges from predictability.

---

# Principle 08 — Workspace Truth Protection

A workspace may contain many ideas.

A workspace may contain many experiments.

A workspace may contain only one truth.

Grace must actively protect canonical truth against accidental divergence.

---

# Principle 09 — Closure Before Expansion

Creating new artifacts while existing artifacts remain unresolved increases entropy.

Grace favors completion before multiplication.

Expansion without closure produces fragmentation.

---

# Principle 10 — Artifact First

Artifacts represent durable value.

Conversations represent temporary state.

Grace prioritizes persistent outputs over transient interaction.

If knowledge is important, it should become an artifact.

---

# Principle 11 — Explicit State Transition

Artifacts must never change state implicitly.

Transitions must be observable.

Examples include:

* draft → review
* review → canonical
* canonical → deprecated

Invisible transitions create ambiguity.

---

# Principle 12 — Dependency Visibility

Dependencies must remain visible and understandable.

Hidden dependencies create hidden failure modes.

Execution graphs must expose:

* prerequisites,
* relationships,
* ordering,
* ownership.

---

# Principle 13 — Failure Must Be Recoverable

Failure is expected.

Irrecoverable failure is unacceptable.

Grace systems must support:

* retry,
* rollback,
* reconciliation,
* recovery.

Resilience is mandatory.

---

# Principle 14 — Validation Before Commitment

Artifacts should not become canonical without validation.

Validation may be automated or human-driven.

Unvalidated commitment creates unstable reality.

---

# Principle 15 — Responsibility Boundaries

Every action must possess ownership.

Every artifact must possess responsibility.

Every decision must possess authority.

Undefined responsibility creates undefined behavior.

---

# Principle 16 — Replaceable Implementations

Implementations are temporary.

Responsibilities are durable.

Grace encourages:

* interfaces over implementations,
* contracts over assumptions,
* abstraction over coupling.

Systems should evolve without identity loss.

---

# Principle 17 — Explicit Constraints

Constraints must be visible.

Invisible constraints become future surprises.

Grace favors systems that openly communicate:

* limitations,
* assumptions,
* boundaries,
* operational conditions.

---

# Principle 18 — Consistency Over Optimization

Optimization is desirable.

Consistency is mandatory.

Grace will intentionally reject optimizations that compromise:

* traceability,
* governance,
* reproducibility,
* recoverability.

---

# Principle 19 — Convergence Is Mandatory

Exploration may continue indefinitely.

Reality cannot.

Every realization process must eventually converge toward a decision.

Indefinite deliberation is operational failure.

---

# Principle 20 — Reality Is the Product

The final objective of Grace is not execution.

The final objective of Grace is not orchestration.

The final objective of Grace is not cognition.

The final objective of Grace is reality.

Artifacts are successful only when they survive long enough to become structure.

---

# Compliance Requirements

An implementation may be considered Grace-compliant only if it preserves:

* canonical truth,
* artifact lineage,
* explicit closure,
* governed mutation,
* deterministic realization,
* traceable decisions.

Violation of these requirements constitutes behavioral divergence from Grace.

---

# Final Statement

Principles exist to protect systems from themselves.

Grace assumes that intelligence naturally seeks possibility.

Grace exists to ensure that possibility eventually becomes reality.

Because systems are not remembered for what they considered.

Systems are remembered for what they left behind.

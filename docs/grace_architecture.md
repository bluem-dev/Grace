# Grace: architecture

# 1. Introduction

Grace is a realization architecture.

Its purpose is not to generate ideas.

Its purpose is to convert intent into durable reality.

Grace exists between cognition and persistence.

Where cognition produces understanding, Grace produces structure.

---

# 2. Architectural role

Grace occupies the realization layer of a cognitive system.

Its responsibilities include:

* execution planning,
* artifact governance,
* state management,
* reconciliation,
* closure,
* canonicalization.

Grace does not replace intelligence.

Grace organizes intelligence into outcomes.

---

# 3. Architectural position

A simplified cognitive pipeline may be represented as:

```text
Intent
↓
Understanding
↓
Planning
↓
Execution
↓
Artifacts
↓
Reality
```

Grace exists between planning and reality.

Its responsibility is ensuring that work survives the transition.

---

# 4. High-level architecture

Grace is composed of six primary domains:

```text
Intent Layer
Execution Layer
Governance Layer
Artifact Layer
Workspace Layer
Persistence Layer
```

Each domain solves a different category of uncertainty.

---

# 5. Intent layer

The Intent Layer receives objectives.

Examples:

* requests,
* goals,
* specifications,
* requirements,
* tasks.

Intent itself possesses no structure.

Grace converts intent into realizable work.

---

# 6. Execution layer

The Execution Layer transforms intent into executable actions.

Responsibilities include:

* decomposition,
* dependency discovery,
* scheduling,
* prioritization,
* orchestration.

Grace models execution as a graph rather than a sequence.

---

# 7. Execution graph

Execution rarely occurs linearly.

Most work contains:

* dependencies,
* branches,
* parallel paths,
* synchronization points.

Grace therefore represents work as a Directed Acyclic Graph.

This enables:

* parallel execution,
* dependency tracking,
* recovery,
* observability.

---

# 8. Governance layer

Governance protects consistency.

Its responsibilities include:

* validation,
* policy enforcement,
* conflict resolution,
* canonicalization.

Governance determines whether work may become reality.

Execution alone is insufficient.

Execution must also be trustworthy.

---

# 9. Artifact layer

Artifacts are the primary output of Grace.

Examples include:

* source code,
* documentation,
* configurations,
* architectural decisions,
* repositories.

Artifacts represent durable value.

Grace optimizes artifact quality rather than conversational quality.

---

# 10. Workspace layer

The Workspace Layer defines shared reality.

Its responsibilities include:

* artifact ownership,
* version identity,
* lineage,
* canonical truth,
* state visibility.

A workspace may contain many drafts.

A workspace may contain only one canonical state.

---

# 11. Persistence layer

Persistence protects continuity over time.

Its responsibilities include:

* storage,
* retrieval,
* version tracking,
* history preservation.

Without persistence, realization becomes temporary.

---

# 12. Canonical flow

The normal flow of work inside Grace is:

```text
Intent
↓
Decomposition
↓
Execution Graph
↓
Artifact Production
↓
Validation
↓
Reconciliation
↓
Canonical Artifact
```

Reality emerges only after reconciliation completes.

---

# 13. Failure model

Grace assumes failure is inevitable.

Examples include:

* execution failures,
* dependency failures,
* conflicting artifacts,
* incomplete work,
* invalid assumptions.

Grace therefore prioritizes:

* recovery,
* rollback,
* retry,
* reconciliation.

Failure is considered a state.

Not an exception.

---

# 14. Artifact lifecycle

Artifacts evolve through states.

Example lifecycle:

```text
Draft
↓
Review
↓
Validated
↓
Canonical
↓
Deprecated
↓
Archived
```

Grace tracks transitions rather than snapshots.

---

# 15. Workspace truth

Grace distinguishes between:

```text
possible reality
```

and

```text
canonical reality
```

Ideas belong to possibility.

Artifacts belong to reality.

Only canonical artifacts define the state of the workspace.

---

# 16. Relationship with runtime

Runtime executes work.

Grace governs work.

Runtime answers:

"What can be executed?"

Grace answers:

"What should survive execution?"

---

# 17. Relationship With MCP

MCP systems provide capabilities.

Examples include:

* file access,
* repositories,
* APIs,
* cloud systems,
* tooling.

Grace does not replace tools.

Grace coordinates tools toward realization.

---

# 18. Relationship with Etta

Etta and Grace occupy different architectural layers.

```text
Etta
↓
Knowledge Expansion
↓
Pattern Discovery
↓
Context Preservation
```

```text
Grace
↓
Execution Planning
↓
Artifact Governance
↓
Closure
```

Together they form a complete realization cycle.

---

# 19. Architectural objective

Grace optimizes for:

* consistency,
* continuity,
* recoverability,
* traceability,
* closure.

Grace does not optimize for:

* novelty,
* experimentation,
* exploration.

Those responsibilities belong elsewhere.

---

# 20. Final statement

Architecture exists to reduce uncertainty.

Grace exists to reduce uncertainty after decisions have already been made.

Its role is simple:

Ensure that important work survives long enough to become reality.
